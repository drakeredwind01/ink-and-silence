## Gemini

The short answer is: Yes, mostly. In the wild, both killer whales (orcas) and false killer whales
are generally indifferent or even friendly toward humans. However, their "kindness" is usually
closer to intense curiosity or indifference rather than the cuddly vibe we associate with a
golden retriever.

**1. Killer Whales (Orcas)**

Despite the "killer" in their name, wild orcas have a remarkably clean record when it comes to
humans.

- Wild Behavior: There are zero confirmed fatal attacks by orcas on humans in the wild. While
  they are terrifyingly efficient at hunting great white sharks and blue whales, they don't seem
  to view humans as food.
- Curiosity: They are highly intelligent and social. They've been known to follow boats,
  "spy-hop" (pop their heads out of the water) to look at people, and even interact playfully
  with divers.
- Mistaken Identity: There is one famous case of a surfer being bitten in 1972, but experts
  believe the orca likely mistook him for a seal (it let go immediately.)
- The Captivity Factor: Almost all aggressive or fatal interactions between orcas and humans
  have occurred in captivity. Scientists believe the stress of confined environments can cause
  these highly social animals to lash out — a behavior rarely seen in the open ocean.

**2. False Killer Whales**

These guys are actually members of the dolphin family, and they take the "friendly dolphin"
trope to a whole new level.

- The "Gift-Givers": False killer whales are famously social and have been observed offering
  "gifts" to humans, such as bringing freshly caught fish to divers or boats.
- Zero Aggression: There are no recorded instances of false killer whales killing or seriously
  harming humans in the wild. They are known to be incredibly gracious and often intermingle
  with other species, including bottlenose dolphins.
- Social Eating: Unlike orcas, which can be very protective of their kill, false killer whales
  are known to share their food with their pod members — and occasionally try to share it with us.

**3. The "Chill" Factor: Whale Sharks**

Whale sharks are "chill" because they are filter feeders with tiny teeth. They don't have the
predatory drive that orcas do. Interestingly, orcas have recently been filmed hunting whale
sharks — so while the whale shark is chill with humans, the orca is definitely not chill with
the whale shark.
